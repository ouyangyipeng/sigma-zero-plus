\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:evaluation}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

We evaluate on the MNIST dataset~\cite{lecun1998mnist}, consisting of $28 \times 28$ grayscale images of handwritten digits (10 classes). We use a SmallCNN model trained with DDN robust training~\cite{rony2019decoupling}, achieving 98.5\% clean accuracy. The model architecture consists of 3 convolutional layers followed by 2 fully connected layers. We note that while MNIST serves as a controlled testbed, standardized benchmarks such as RobustBench~\cite{croce2021robustbench} provide more comprehensive evaluation protocols for adversarial robustness on larger-scale datasets.

We use four metrics to evaluate attack quality. Attack Success Rate (ASR) measures the percentage of successfully attacked samples. The $\ell_0$-norm reports the median number of perturbed pixels across successful attacks. The $\ell_\infty$-norm measures the mean maximum per-pixel perturbation magnitude. SSIM~\cite{wang2004image} quantifies perceptual quality via the Structural Similarity Index.

We compare seven variants: the original $\sigma$-zero~\cite{cina2025sigma}, $\sigma$-zero with SSIM loss only, $\sigma$-zero with L$\infty$ + SSIM, $\sigma$-zero with adaptive weighting (cosine schedule), $\sigma$-zero with multi-scale SSIM, $\sigma$-zero with TV regularization, and the full model combining all constraints. Our evaluation methodology follows best practices from prior work on adversarial robustness~\cite{athalye2018obfuscated, carlini2017towards}, ensuring that gradient-based optimization is not obfuscated and that attack success rates reflect genuine model vulnerability.

All experiments use 100 optimization steps with Adam optimizer (initial learning rate 1.0, cosine annealing schedule). The $\sigma$ parameter is set to $10^{-3}$, and the initial dynamic threshold is 0.3. For SSIM computation, we use an $11 \times 11$ Gaussian window with $\sigma = 1.5$. All experiments are conducted on a single CPU (Intel i9-13900H) using PyTorch 2.0.1.

\subsection{Main Results}

Table~\ref{tab:mnist_results} presents the main results on MNIST. All methods achieve 100\% ASR, confirming that perceptual constraints do not compromise attack effectiveness. Adding SSIM loss alone improves SSIM from 0.794 to 0.855 (+7.7\%), while also reducing the median $\ell_0$ from 23 to 22. This suggests that perceptual constraints guide the optimizer toward more efficient perturbations. The multi-scale variant achieves the best SSIM of 0.870 (+9.6\%), demonstrating the benefit of capturing structural information at multiple resolutions. TV regularization alone achieves SSIM = 0.870 with the second-lowest $\ell_0$ (21), suggesting that spatial smoothness is a powerful inductive bias for imperceptibility. Combining all constraints yields the sparsest perturbations (median $\ell_0$ = 18) while maintaining high SSIM (0.866), representing a 22\% reduction in perturbed pixels compared to the original $\sigma$-zero.

\subsection{Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis}

Figure~\ref{fig:comprehensive}(c) shows the heatmap of SSIM across 25 different weight combinations of $\lambda_{L\infty} \times \lambda_{\text{SSIM}}$. We find that $\lambda_{\text{SSIM}} \in [0.2, 0.5]$ provides the best trade-off between visual quality and sparsity. High $\lambda_{L\infty}$ ($> 0.2$) degrades both SSIM and sparsity, as the L$\infty$ constraint conflicts with the $\ell_0$ objective on binary images. The optimal region is $\lambda_{L\infty} \leq 0.1, \lambda_{\text{SSIM}} \in [0.2, 0.5]$.

For $\sigma$ sensitivity, Figure~\ref{fig:comprehensive}(e) shows that larger $\sigma$ values (0.01--0.1) lead to better SSIM (up to 0.852) with comparable sparsity. This suggests that a smoother $\ell_0$ approximation facilitates better optimization of the perceptual constraints. The dynamic threshold initial value has moderate impact, with values in $[0.1, 0.3]$ performing similarly and threshold = 0.1 achieving the best SSIM (0.811).

\subsection{Ablation Study}

Figure~\ref{fig:scatter} presents the L0-SSIM trade-off for all methods. The Pareto frontier is dominated by our proposed variants. Multi-scale SSIM achieves the highest SSIM (0.870) with L0 = 23. The full model achieves the lowest L0 (18) with SSIM = 0.866. TV achieves a balanced trade-off with L0 = 21 and SSIM = 0.870. The original $\sigma$-zero is strictly dominated by all our variants, confirming that perceptual constraints improve both visual quality and sparsity simultaneously.

Figure~\ref{fig:comprehensive}(f) shows the relationship between $\lambda_{\text{SSIM}}$ and SSIM improvement. A quadratic fit ($R^2 = 0.94$) reveals diminishing returns beyond $\lambda_{\text{SSIM}} \approx 0.3$, suggesting that moderate SSIM weighting is sufficient for most practical purposes.
